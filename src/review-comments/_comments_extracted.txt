1st Reviewer’s comment:
The core idea is to combine multi-objective student success prediction with SHAP-based, personalized recommendations focused on modifiable factors. This is practically valuable for academic advising and compares several ML and DL models fairly. However, the contribution is more integrative than fundamentally novel, the intervention logic is not causally validated, and reproducibility details are incomplete, especially for preprocessing, tuning, and data availability. 
The problem definition of this work is not clear. In Sect. 2, the drawbacks (Not limitation = research depth) of each conventional technique should be described. The authors should emphasize the difference with other methods to clarify the position of this work further.
To help readers’ understanding, the authors should add a notation list, because there are many variables in equations.
The presentation of figures is not professional. In figures, letters are small and blurry. The authors should enlarge or redraw figures. See Fig. 3, 5, 7, etc. The proposed presentation does not respect the elementary rules of a scientific writing.
The reviewer fails to understand the effectiveness of the proposed technique, because no comparison data with state-of-the-art methods is shown in this paper. In the comparison part, the authors should demonstrate the comparison data to confirm the validity of the obtained results. In-depth comparison and discussion with state-of-the-art methods proposed in 2024-2025 are necessary to clarify the effectiveness.
The manuscript states that the dataset contains “50+ features” in the Abstract, but later states that “43 features span seven categories.” Table 2 also reports category counts summing to 40, not 43. The authors should reconcile the total number of raw features, engineered features, and final model-input features.
Table 2 lists feature-category counts as 3 + 3 + 9 + 10 + 8 + 3 + 4 = 40, while the Total row reports 43. This is a direct numerical inconsistency. Please correct either the category counts or the total.
The recommendation-feature taxonomy reports 16 modifiable, 6 contextual, and 19 immutable features, which sum to 41, not 43. This conflicts with the stated final feature count. Please clarify whether two features are unclassified or whether one of these numbers is incorrect.
The text states that academic_index with SHAP = 0.126 is “3.800d7” more influential than tenth_percentage with SHAP = 0.033. The numerical ratio is approximately 3.82. The expression “3.800d7” appears erroneous and should be corrected.
The text states that workshop participation with SHAP = 0.331 has a SHAP impact “6.600d7” larger than teamwork skills with SHAP = 0.050. The numerical ratio is approximately 6.62. The expression “6.600d7” appears erroneous and should be corrected.
The manuscript states that transformer-based models have F1 = 0.41–0.58, but Table 4 shows the relevant FT-Transformer and SAINT F1-macro values range from 0.423 to 0.584. The rounded range should be reported consistently, for example 0.42–0.58.
2nd Reviewer’s comment:
The paper proposes a multi-objective XAI framework for student success prediction, covering CGPA category, at-risk status, and career readiness. It compares classical ML and tabular DL models, finds XGBoost strongest, and uses SHAP values to generate personalized recommendations based on modifiable student factors.
The study does not directly compare the proposed method with the latest 2024+ XAI-based student performance or dropout prediction frameworks on a common dataset, common split, or reimplemented protocol. The literature table lists recent methods, but the experimental section evaluates only generic baselines and tabular architectures. This weakens the claim that the framework advances the state of the art. The authors should either remove superiority implications or add reproducible comparisons with recent SHAP-based ensemble, LightGBM/XGBoost, imbalance-learning, and recommendation-oriented EDM methods under the same evaluation protocol.
The recommendation engine treats SHAP attribution as if it can support intervention prioritization, but SHAP only explains model predictions and does not establish causal effects. Recommending workshop participation, socialization, or stress coping as high-impact interventions may be misleading unless validated longitudinally or causally. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but the main claims still overstate practical effectiveness. The authors should reframe recommendations as hypothesis-generating decision support and add causal validation, propensity-score analysis, ablation, or prospective intervention evidence.
Reproducibility is insufficient for a middle-tier empirical ML paper. Although the paper reports CV, SMOTE, Optuna, and some model settings, it omits the complete hyperparameter search spaces, final best parameters per objective, preprocessing mappings, feature-generation code, SMOTE parameters, fold indices, software versions, and exact availability conditions for the dataset. “Available upon request” is weaker than reproducible release. The authors should provide a complete replication package or, at minimum, pseudo-code and configuration tables sufficient to reproduce all tables.
There is a potential target-leakage concern. CGPA and backlog-related variables are used to define academic and at-risk labels, while academic_index includes CGPA and prior academic marks. If CGPA or strongly label-defining variables remain in predictors for CGPA category or at-risk prediction, the task may become partially circular. The authors must clearly list excluded target-derived variables for each objective and report leakage-free ablations.
The fairness and generalizability analysis is underdeveloped. Gender appears among top SHAP features, but the paper does not evaluate subgroup performance, calibration, disparate error rates, or whether recommendations differ unfairly across demographic or socioeconomic groups. Since this is an educational decision-support system, fairness analysis is not optional. The authors should report subgroup F1, false-negative rates for at-risk students, calibration, and mitigation strategies.
The comparison with deep learning models may not be fully fair. The dataset is small, but the manuscript does not provide enough detail on optimization, early stopping, batch size, epochs, learning-rate schedules, categorical embeddings, or regularization for TabNet, FT-Transformer, and SAINT. Poor DL performance may reflect implementation or tuning limitations rather than a reliable conclusion. The authors should include full DL training settings and comparable tuning budgets.
