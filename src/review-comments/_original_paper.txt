Abstract: Proactive identification of students who need academic support, combined with personalized intervention strategies, is essential for improving outcomes in higher education. This study proposes an end-to-end Explainable AI (XAI) framework that addresses three critical educational objectives simultaneously: (1) academic performance assessment—classifying students into Low, Medium, and High CGPA categories; (2) at-risk student identification—detecting students likely to struggle academically (CGPA below 7.0 or carrying backlogs); and (3) career readiness evaluation—assessing whether a student is prepared to start their professional career, measured through completion of internships and academic projects. Using a dataset of 1,378 engineering students with 52 raw survey features that, after preprocessing and engineering, yield 42 model-input features spanning academic, demographic, behavioral, and socioeconomic dimensions, we develop a feature engineering pipeline that creates composite indicators (academic_index, skills_index, engagement_score) and interaction features. Seven models—four machine learning (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, SVM, XGBoost) and three deep learning (TabNet, FT-Transformer, SAINT)—are systematically compared using 5-fold stratified cross-validation with SMOTE-based class imbalance handling and Optuna hyperparameter optimization. XGBoost achieves the best performance with F1-macro of 0.805 (±0.017) for academic assessment, 0.767 (±0.040) for at-risk identification, and 0.699 (±0.024) for career readiness. SHAP (SHapley Additive explanations) analysis reveals that the engineered academic_index is the strongest predictor of academic outcomes, while engagement_score drives career readiness. A SHAP-based recommendation engine translates these insights into personalized, hypothesis-generating interventions—for example, workshop participation shows a SHAP impact of +0.331 for at-risk students, making it the highest-leverage intervention. The framework provides educators and academic advisors with transparent, evidence-based tools for student support.

1. Introduction
1.1 Background and Motivation
The rapid expansion of higher education enrollment globally has created unprecedented challenges for academic institutions in monitoring, supporting, and guiding individual student performance. According to UNESCO, global tertiary enrollment surpassed 235 million students in 2023, placing immense pressure on institutional resources for academic advising and support services. Traditional approaches to student monitoring—relying on aggregate statistics, periodic grade reviews, and manual advisor interventions—fail to capture the complex, multi-factorial nature of student success.
Machine learning (ML) offers a data-driven alternative capable of identifying patterns in student data that human advisors might miss. However, recent systematic reviews of educational prediction models show that many educational data mining (EDM) studies remain “prediction-focused”—building models that predict outcomes without explaining the underlying factors. This “black box” approach fundamentally limits the practical utility of predictive models in education, where stakeholders (faculty, advisors, and administrators) need to understand why a student is at risk before they can design effective interventions [1].
Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) addresses this critical limitation by combining predictive power with interpretability. Recent explainable dropout and student-performance studies use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to provide feature attributions at both global (population-level) and local (individual student) levels, making it particularly suitable for educational applications where personalized understanding is paramount [2]. Unlike simpler feature importance methods (e.g., Gini importance in Random Forest), SHAP-style explanations support local accuracy, consistency, and additivity—properties essential for trustworthy educational decision support.
1.2 Problem Statement
Student success is inherently multi-dimensional, yet existing prediction systems typically address only a single aspect. A comprehensive student support framework must simultaneously answer three questions that educators face daily:
How is this student performing academically? Accurate classification of students by academic performance level (e.g., CGPA categories) enables differentiated support—remedial assistance for struggling students and enrichment opportunities for high performers.
Is this student at risk of academic failure? Early identification of at-risk students—those with declining grades or accumulated backlogs—enables proactive intervention before academic problems become irreversible.
Is this student prepared for their professional career? Evaluating career readiness—whether students have completed essential professional development milestones such as internships and academic projects—helps career services provide targeted guidance to those who need it most.
Furthermore, predictions alone are insufficient. Educators require two additional capabilities: (a) transparent explanations of which factors drive each prediction, and (b) actionable recommendations specifying which modifiable student behaviors to target for improvement. Current systems provide predictions but leave educators to independently determine appropriate interventions—a gap this work explicitly addresses through a SHAP-based recommendation engine.
1.3 Research Objectives
This study addresses these challenges through three objectives:
To propose a multi-objective performance prediction framework using data-driven target selection and Explainable AI, simultaneously predicting academic performance, at-risk status, and career readiness.
To design and implement a SHAP-based recommendation engine that generates personalized, actionable suggestions targeting only modifiable student factors, with quantified expected impact.
To systematically compare machine learning and deep learning approaches on educational tabular data, providing empirical guidance for model selection in educational contexts.
1.4 Key Contributions
The principal contributions of this work are:
Multi-objective prediction framework: A unified framework addressing three actionable educational objectives—academic performance assessment (F1=0.805), at-risk identification (F1=0.767), and career readiness evaluation (F1=0.699)—each serving a distinct stakeholder need. To our knowledge, no prior study simultaneously addresses all three objectives.
Domain-informed feature engineering: Composite scores (academic_index, skills_index, engagement_score) and interaction features (study_stress_interaction, skills_study_interaction) that emerged as the strongest predictors across all objectives, demonstrating that domain knowledge-driven feature design substantially outperforms raw survey features.
SHAP-based recommendation engine: An automated system (Algorithm 1) that translates SHAP-derived feature attributions into personalized, actionable interventions. The engine uses a novel feasibility-weighted ranking score that balances feature impact with improvement headroom, and includes edge-case handling for uniform SHAP values and already-optimal students.
Comprehensive ML vs. DL benchmark: Systematic evaluation of seven models—four ML (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, SVM, XGBoost) and three DL (TabNet, FT-Transformer, SAINT)—with Optuna Bayesian hyperparameter optimization, providing empirical evidence that XGBoost remains optimal for small educational tabular datasets ( 1,400 records).
Reproducible end-to-end pipeline: A complete, open framework with documented random seeds covering data preprocessing, feature engineering, model training, explainability analysis, recommendation generation, and ready visualization.

2. Literature Review
2.1 Student Performance Prediction Using Machine Learning
Chen et al. [3] proposed an educational-data-mining approach for student performance prediction in IEEE Access, reinforcing the value of structured academic and behavioral features for early academic analytics. Recent systematic reviews by Al-Din and Al Abdulqader [4] and Kumari et al. [5] similarly report that tree-based and ensemble methods remain dominant in educational prediction studies, especially when institutional datasets contain mixed numerical and categorical variables.
Alamgir et al. [6] showed that educational data mining on academic records can improve student-performance prediction when features are selected and evaluated carefully. Lou and Colvin [7] compared educational data mining methods on a large K-12 dataset and found that method choice depends strongly on the target outcome and data characteristics. Saeed [8] and Tao-Hongli [9] further emphasized feature selection and model evaluation as central steps in reliable educational prediction pipelines.
Sarker et al. [10] analyzed student academic performance using educational data mining and highlighted the continued importance of interpretable predictors for institutional decision-making. Bettahi et al. [11] proposed a modular explainable dropout-prediction pipeline for higher education, while Yu et al. [12] demonstrated that semi-supervised feature selection can improve educational data mining for performance analysis.
A common drawback of the gradient-boosted and ensemble pipelines surveyed here is that they treat student outcomes as a single scalar target (CGPA or pass/fail), which conflates academic performance with at-risk status and career readiness. They also rely on raw survey features and offer no automatic translation from feature attribution to advisor-facing recommendation. Our work removes both restrictions by adopting (i) a unified multi-objective formulation in which each task has its own loss function and feature taxonomy, and (ii) a SHAP-driven recommendation engine that maps attributions to modifiable behaviors.
2.2 Explainable AI in Education
The demand for transparency in educational AI has driven significant adoption of XAI techniques. Almalawi et al. [1] reviewed predictive models for educational purposes and identified interpretability as a recurring requirement for practical adoption. Kruger et al. [2] demonstrated an explainable machine-learning approach for student dropout prediction, showing how model explanations can support institutional interventions.
Kalita et al. [13] combined Bi-LSTM prediction with SHAP-based interpretability and statistical validation for academic performance prediction. Paul et al. [14] analyzed student dropout and explainable academic-performance prediction using AI techniques, while Liu et al. [15] integrated ensemble learning with SHAP-based explainable AI for dropout analysis.
Abukader et al. [16] proposed a metaheuristic-optimized LightGBM system with SHAP-based learning analytics for student performance prediction. Cabral et al. [17] reviewed AI-powered learning analytics dashboards and highlighted explainability and ethics as open challenges, while Takami et al. [18] developed personality-based explainable recommendations for trustworthy smart learning systems.
A consistent finding across these studies is that while SHAP explanations improve interpretability, no existing system automatically translates SHAP values into personalized, actionable recommendations—leaving a critical "last mile" gap between explanation and intervention.
The principal drawback of existing XAI-in-EDM systems is that they stop at post-hoc explanation — SHAP plots and feature importances are displayed, but no system automatically converts those attributions into actionable, personalized recommendations or audits the recommendations for fairness across demographic subgroups. Our work closes this loop with Algorithm 1 and adds the per-subgroup F1, FNR, and calibration audit reported in section 5.6.
2.3 Deep Learning for Tabular Educational Data
Recent years have seen renewed interest in neural architectures for tabular data. Lin et al. [19] proposed Tab2Text as a framework for deep learning with tabular data, while Goldblum et al. [20] investigated when neural networks outperform boosted trees on tabular data. Hollmann et al. [21] introduced a tabular foundation model for accurate predictions on small data, showing that foundation-style tabular learning is becoming increasingly competitive.
Despite these advances, the application of modern tabular DL architectures to educational data mining remains limited, particularly for small survey-style datasets. Fan and Waldmann [22] provided a comparative study of tabular deep learning architectures, and Yi et al. [23] demonstrated how XGBoost-based SHAP analysis can support interpretable prediction in an educational assessment setting. These recent studies motivate a direct benchmark between optimized classical ML and DL-style tabular models in the present work.
Modern tabular-DL architectures (TabNet, FT-Transformer, SAINT) rely on large feature interactions that small educational survey datasets (typically <2 000 records) cannot reliably support. The drawback for the EDM community is that recent benchmarks underplay this small-N regime, leading practitioners to over-invest in DL architectures rather than in domain-informed feature engineering. Our work establishes — under matched 50-trial Optuna budgets and identical CV protocol — that XGBoost dominates this regime and that the locus of improvement is feature engineering, not architecture.

3.3 Data Preprocessing Pipeline
The dataset used in this study is a custom dataset created by the authors through a structured survey of 1,378 engineering students across five institutions affiliated with universities in Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh, India. Data collection was conducted between July and September 2024 using validated Google Forms with built-in validation rules and mandatory field requirements. The survey instrument was developed through faculty expert consultation and validated by a certified psychiatrist for psychosocial scales (Cronbach's α > 0.78). Informed consent was obtained electronically, and all responses were anonymized.
The dataset encompasses 52 features spanning academic performance (10th%, 12th%, CGPA, backlogs), demographics (gender, age, residence), family background (parental education, occupation, income), behavioral factors (study hours, extracurriculars, workshops), psychosocial indicators (communication, leadership, teamwork, stress, health on validated 1–5 Likert scales), and career outcomes. With only 0.29% missing data (4 values total), the dataset provides high-quality real-world student data.
Preprocessing included: (a) standardization of inconsistent binary encodings and education labels across columns, (b) median imputation for the 4 missing numeric values, (c) ordinal encoding preserving natural category hierarchies, (d) binary 0,1 mapping for Yes/No columns, (e) label encoding for nominal categoricals, and (f) z-score normalization of continuous numerical features.

3.10 Leakage Controls
Because the at-risk and CGPA-category labels are deterministic functions of current_cgpa and backlog_status, and the career-readiness label is a deterministic function of internship_completion and academic_projects_completed_yn, retaining these raw columns as predictors would induce target leakage. We therefore apply the following discipline enforced in experiment code: 
Target-defining raw columns are dropped from the predictor set for every objective. The dropped set is current_cgpa, backlog_status, internship_completion, academic_projects_completed_yn, internship_payment_status, placement_status, higher_education_interest_yn.
Engineered features are constructed without target-defining inputs. academic_index uses only tenth_percentage and twelth_percentage; engagement_score uses only extracurricular_support, workshop_seminar_participation_yn, and community_service_yn. The remaining engineered features (skills_index, study_stress_interaction, skills_study_interaction) have no overlap with any target.
SMOTE is applied only inside training folds. Validation and test folds never see synthetic minority samples, preventing the optimistic-bias mode reported in [24].

The Optuna budget for each DL architecture is identical to that for XGBoost (50 trials × 3 inner folds = 150 inner-CV evaluations), so the gap between XGBoost and the best DL model on this dataset cannot be attributed to under-tuning of the DL architectures. The persistence of that gap (Table 4) is consistent with recent results showing that gradient-boosted trees remain dominant on small tabular data when the ratio of unique feature interactions to sample count is low [20], [21]. We therefore frame the DL underperformance as regime-specific to small-N educational survey data, not as a categorical claim about tabular DL.

5.5 Causal Plausibility via Propensity-Score Matching
SHAP attributions are associational and cannot, on their own, support intervention prioritization. To probe whether the SHAP-derived rankings are at least consistent with directional causal effects on current_cgpa, we conducted a propensity-score-matched (PSM) analysis on the three highest-ranked modifiable features identified by Algorithm 1: workshop / seminar participation (binary), high peer-group quality (Likert ≥ 4), and stress-coping (binary).
Method. For each treatment, propensity P(T=1 | X) is estimated via logistic regression on the leakage-free confounder matrix (all features except the treatment, the outcome, and the seven target-derived columns listed in §3.10). 1:1 nearest-neighbour matching is performed on the logit of the propensity using a caliper of 0.2 SD. The Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT) is the mean within-pair difference in current_cgpa, with a 95 % bootstrap CI (1 000 paired resamples).

5.6 Discussion
The results reveal several important insights for the EDM community:
Feature engineering is more impactful than model complexity. The engineered academic_index and engagement_score consistently rank as top predictors across all objectives, outperforming any individual raw feature. This suggests that domain-informed composite indicators capture student characteristics more effectively than individual survey responses. For instance, academic_index (SHAP=0.126 for at-risk) is approximately 3.82× more influential than the next raw feature (tenth_percentage, SHAP=0.033). This finding has practical implications: researchers should invest more effort in domain-driven feature engineering than in trying increasingly complex model architectures.
Multi-objective framing enables holistic student support. By predicting academic performance, at-risk status, and career readiness simultaneously, the framework enables institutions to provide differentiated support: academic tutoring for CGPA-focused intervention, proactive counseling for at-risk students, and career development guidance for career readiness gaps. A student may perform adequately on CGPA (predicted Medium) yet be flagged as not career-ready due to missing internship experience—an insight invisible to single-objective systems.
Gradient boosting remains optimal for small educational tabular data. Despite architectural innovations in TabNet, FT-Transformer, and SAINT, none matches XGBoost's combination of high accuracy, low variance, and computational efficiency on this 1,378-record dataset. The performance gap is substantial (F1: 0.805 vs. 0.645 for the best DL model on CGPA). This aligns with recent tabular-data findings by Goldblum et al. [20], who analyzed when neural networks outperform boosted trees. Practitioners working with typical institutional survey datasets (<5,000 records) should prioritize XGBoost with SHAP over DL architectures.
Behavioral factors are key intervention targets for at-risk students. While academic history (captured by academic_index) is the strongest predictor, behavioral factors that students can change—workshop participation (SHAP +0.331), socializing patterns, stress coping—rank among the top modifiable features. This validates the recommendation engine's approach of targeting modifiable factors: the highest-impact intervention (workshop participation) has a SHAP impact approximately 6.62× larger than the fifth-ranked factor (teamwork skills), enabling clear prioritization of institutional resources.
Career readiness prediction confirms engagement as the primary driver. The F1 of 0.699 for career readiness, driven primarily by engagement_score, suggests that career outcomes for engineering students are strongly determined by active participation in professional development activities. This finding supports institutional policies that mandate internship completion and project-based learning as degree requirements.
5.7 Threats to Validity
Internal validity: The cross-sectional survey design captures associations but not causal relationships. SHAP attributions identify predictive factors, not interventions proven to improve outcomes. The recommendation that "workshop participation improves at-risk status" is a hypothesis generated by the model, not a validated causal claim. Longitudinal or experimental validation is needed to confirm intervention effectiveness.
External validity: The dataset represents engineering students from five institutions in Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh, India. Results may not generalize to other disciplines, countries, or educational systems. The demographic composition (58.4% male, predominantly 18–25 age group) reflects the engineering education context in India but limits generalizability to more diverse populations.
Construct validity: Self-reported behavioral and psychosocial features (study hours, stress frequency, skill ratings) may be subject to social desirability bias. However, the validated survey instrument (Cronbach's 03b1 > 0.78) and anonymized collection process mitigate this concern. The career readiness definition (internship + projects completed) is a proxy measure; actual career success depends on additional factors not captured in the dataset.
Statistical conclusion validity: All results are reported with standard deviation across 5-fold cross-validation, and class imbalance is explicitly handled via SMOTE and class-weighted loss. The sample size (N=1,378) provides sufficient statistical power for the observed effect sizes, though the minority class in CGPA (Low: 8.1%, n=112) has limited representation per fold.
Causal validity. SHAP attributions are associational and not, on their own, sufficient to support intervention prioritization. The propensity-score-matched analysis in section 5.5 provides a plausibility check showing that the directional signs of the SHAP-derived recommendations are consistent with directional ATT estimates after controlling for measured confounders, but observational matching cannot rule out unobserved confounding. Recommendations should therefore be interpreted as hypothesis-generating decision support for academic advisors, not as established causal interventions. Validation requires longitudinal panel data or randomized cluster-level interventions, identified as principal future-work items in section 6.
6. Conclusion
This study presents an Explainable AI framework for multi-objective student performance prediction addressing three critical educational needs: academic performance assessment (XGBoost F1=0.805), at-risk student identification (F1=0.767), and career readiness evaluation (F1=0.699).
The principal findings are: (1) domain-informed feature engineering produces composite indicators (academic_index, engagement_score) that outperform all individual raw features as predictors across all three objectives; (2) XGBoost with SHAP provides the optimal combination of prediction accuracy and interpretability for small educational tabular datasets, outperforming three DL architectures (TabNet, FT-Transformer, SAINT); (3) the SHAP-based recommendation engine successfully translates feature attributions into prioritized, personalized interventions targeting only modifiable student behaviors; and (4) behavioral factors, particularly workshop participation and peer engagement, emerge as the highest-leverage intervention targets for at-risk students.
The framework's practical significance lies in its ability to simultaneously identify which students need help (at-risk identification), why they are struggling (SHAP explanations), and what specific actions can improve their outcomes (personalized recommendations)—providing a complete decision-support pipeline for academic advisors.
Future work includes: (1) Cluster-randomized intervention trials at the institute level for the top-ranked modifiable features (workshop participation, peer-group quality, stress coping), (2) Longitudinal extension to a multi-semester panel that allows directed acyclic-graph-based causal inference, (3) External validation on multi-institutional and multi-disciplinary datasets across different educational systems, (4) Integration of structural causal-effect estimators (g-formula, doubly-robust ATE estimation), (5) Interactive dashboard development for real-time advisor use with embedded fairness monitors, and (6) Extension to sequential prediction using temporal student data.
